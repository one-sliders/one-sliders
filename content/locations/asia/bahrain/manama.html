City history

410By 410–420 AD, a Nestorian bishopric and monastery was established in Al Dair, on the neighbouring island of Muharraq.
600The yearly income of exported Bahraini pearls was 600,000 ducats, collected by around 2,000 pearling dhows.
1330In 1330, under the Jarwanid dynasty, the island became a tributary of the Kingdom of Hormuz.
1521In 1521, Bahrain fell to the expanding Portuguese Empire in the Persian Gulf, having already defeated Hormuz.
1602After numerous revolts and an expanding Safavid empire in Persia, the Portuguese were expelled from Bahrain and the Safavids took control in 1602.
1736Sometime after 1736, Nader Shah constructed a fort on the southern outskirts of Manama (likely the Diwan Fort).
1869With the ascension of Isa ibn Ali Al Khalifa as the Hakim of Bahrain in 1869, Manama became the centre of British activity in the Persian Gulf, though its interests were initially strictly commercial.
1873Trading recovered fully by 1873 and the country's earnings from pearl exports increased by sevenfold between 1873 and 1900.
1914Following the outbreak of World War I in 1914, the British Raj used Manama as a military base of operations during the Mesopotamian campaign.
1947In 1947, following the end of the war and subsequent Indian independence, the British residency of the Persian Gulf moved to Manama from Bushire.
3000The Dilmun civilisation inhabited the area in 3000 BC, serving as a key regional trading hub between Mesopotamia, Magan and the Indus Valley Civilisation.
Regional roleBahrain, particularly Manama and the nearby settlement of Bilad Al Qadeem, became a centre of Shia scholarship and training for the ulema, it would remain so for centuries.
Regional roleThe Portuguese consolidated their hold on the island by constructing the Bahrain Fort, on the outskirts of Manama.
Regional roleThe Safavids, sidelining Manama, designated the nearby town of Bilad Al Qadeem as the provincial capital.
