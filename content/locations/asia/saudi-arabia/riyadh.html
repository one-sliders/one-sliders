City history

866Al-Yamama broke away from the Abbasid in 866 and the area fell under the rule of the Banu Ukhaidhir, who moved the capital from Hajr to nearby Al-Kharj.
1590The earliest known reference to the area by the name Riyadh comes from a 17th-century chronicler reporting on an event from the year 1590.
1737In 1737, Dahham ibn Dawwas, a refugee from neighboring Manfuhah, took control of Riyadh.
OriginsDuring the Pre-Islamic era, the city at the site of modern Riyadh was called Hajr (Arabic: حجر), and was reportedly founded by the tribe of Banu Hanifa.
14th centuryIn the 14th century, North African traveler Ibn Battuta wrote of his visit to Hajr, describing it as "the main city of Al-Yamama, and its name is Hajr".
GrowthHajr served as the capital of the province of Al-Yamama, whose governors were responsible for most of central and eastern Arabia during the Umayyad Caliphate and Abbasid Caliphate eras.
Regional roleIbn Battuta goes on to describe it as a city of canals and trees with most of its inhabitants belonging to the Banu Hanifa, and reports that he continued on with their leader to.
Regional roleLater on, Hajr broke up into several separate settlements and estates.
Regional roleThe most notable of these were Migrin (or Muqrin) and Mi'kal, though the name Hajr continued to appear in local folk poetry.
Regional roleIbn Dawwas built a single wall to encircle the various oasis towns in the area, making them effectively a single fortress city.
