City history

870Muslims were a minority until Ya'qub bin Laith as-Saffar of Zaranj conquered Kabul in 870 from the Hindu Shahis and established the first Islamic dynasty in the region.
1504In 1504, the city fell to Babur from the north and made into his headquarters, which became one of the principal cities of his later Mughal Empire.
1776Interest in the city was renewed when Ahmad Shah's son Timur Shah Durrani, after inheriting power, transferred the capital of the Durrani Empire from Kandahar to Kabul in 1776.
1903The first modern high school, Habibia, was established in 1903.
20th centuryDuring the war, Bala Hissar was partially destroyed by a fire and an explosion. 20th century In Kabul, an established bazaar city, leather and textile industries developed by 1916.
1919In 1919, after the Third Anglo-Afghan War, King Amanullah Khan announced Afghanistan's independence in foreign affairs at Eidgah Mosque in Kabul.
1960sKabul University opened in 1932, and by the 1960s the majority of teachers were western educated Afghans and the majority of instructors at the university had degrees from Western universities.
1937A radio tower built in Kabul by the Germans in 1937 provided communication with outlying villages.
1955In 1955, the Soviet Union forwarded $100 million in credit to Afghanistan which financed public transportation, airports, a cement factory, a mechanised bakery, a five-lane highway from Kabul to the Soviet border and dams.
2001In 2001 rebuilding began and many of the city's damaged landmarks were rebuilt or restored, including the Gardens of Babur in 2005, the arch of Paghman, the Mahmoud Khan Bridge clock tower in 2013.
14th centuryTheir principal mountain is called Kuh Sulayman. Timurid and Mughal era In the 14th century, Kabul became a major trading centre under the kingdom of Timur (Tamerlane).
19th centuryIn the early 19th century, Kabul and other major Afghan cities became sites of refuge for Jews fleeing persecution in neighbouring Iran.
Regional roleThough Mughal power became centred within the Indian subcontinent, Kabul retained importance as a frontier city for the empire; Abul Fazl, Emperor Akbar's chronicler, described it as one of the two gates to Hindustan.
Regional rolePolitical crime and guerrilla attacks on military and government targets were common, and the sound of gunfire became commonplace at night in the outskirts.
