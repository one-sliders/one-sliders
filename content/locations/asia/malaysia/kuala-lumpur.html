City history

1859Despite a high death toll from the malarial conditions of the jungle, the Ampang mines succeeded and exported the first tin in 1859.
1880In 1880, the colonial administration moved the state capital of Selangor from Klang to the more strategically advantageous Kuala Lumpur, and British Resident William Bloomfield Douglas decided to locate the government buildings and living.
1881In late 1881, the town was severely flooded, after a fire that had destroyed the entire town in January.
1884He also stipulated in 1884 that buildings should be constructed of brick and tile so that they would be less flammable, and that the town be rebuilt with wider streets to reduce fire risk.
1886A railway line between Kuala Lumpur and Klang, initiated by Swettenham and completed in 1886, increased access and resulted in rapid growth.
1897The Padang, now known as Independence Square, would later become the centre of the British administrative offices when the colonial government offices moved to the Sultan Abdul Samad Building in 1897.
1960Kuala Lumpur officially became the capital of the Malayan federation under the Federal Capital Act 1960 remained still after the formation of Malaysia on 16 September 1963.
1820sOne suggestion is that it was named after Sungai Lumpur ("muddy river"); in the 1820s, a place named Sungei Lumpoor was said to be the most important tin-producing settlement on the Klang River.
Regional roleAfter the mines were opened, Raja Abdullah then sent a garrison under the command of a Bugis lieutenant to man a stockade built at Bukit Nanas.
Regional roleImportant Malay figures of early Kuala Lumpur also included Haji Mohamed Tahir, who became the Dato Dagang ("chief of traders").
Regional roleThe Minangkabaus of Sumatra became another important group who traded and established tobacco plantations in the area.
