City history

116The United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs estimated that 116,000 people had been directly affected by the earthquake, equivalent to a third of Vanuatu's population.
1774Efate Island was charted in 1774 by James Cook, who named it Sandwich Island.
1887After 1887, the territory was jointly administered by the French and the British.
1889Instead, it was converted into the municipality of Franceville, which declared independence on 9 August 1889, though this only lasted until June of the following year.
2002A powerful earthquake on 3 January 2002 caused minor damage in the capital and surrounding areas.
2004In the autumn of 2004, an archaeological expedition known as Teouma discovered a burial site of 25 tombs containing three dozen skeletons of members of the Lapita culture.
2015The city suffered massive damage from a category 5 cyclone named Cyclone Pam on 13 March 2015, whose eye wall passed just to the east of Port Vila.
2024On 17 December 2024, a magnitude 7.3 earthquake reportedly damaged almost every single house in Port Vila, resulting in 16 fatalities.
OriginsMelanesian people have occupied the Port Vila area for thousands of years.
19th centuryIn the late 19th century, when the islands were known as the New Hebrides, the British initially had the dominant European presence.
1890sHowever, by the 1890s, the economic balance had begun favouring the French, who established large plantations.
Civic roleCook's expedition did not land but noted the presence of good harbours and land suitable for European settlement.
Regional roleFrench citizen Ferdinand Chevillard began buying and clearing land around Port Vila to be converted into the largest French plantation on the island.
Regional roleDuring World War II, Port Vila was an American and Australian airbase.
