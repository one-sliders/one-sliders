City history

1525Although the Chiriguanos were repelled by commanders sent by Wayna Qhapaq from Quito, the Portuguese conquistador Aleixo Garcia is believed to be the first European to make contact with Charcas in 1525.
1559In 1559, the Spanish King Philip II established the Audiencia de Charcas in La Plata with authority over an area which covers what is now Paraguay, southeastern Peru, northern Chile and Argentina, and much.
1601In 1601 the Recoleta Monastery was founded by the Franciscans.
1624In 1624 St Francis Xavier University of Chuquisaca was founded.
1776The Real Audiencia of Charcas was a subdivision of the Viceroyalty of Peru until 1776, when it was transferred to the newly created Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata.
1809On May 25, 1809, the Bolivian independence movement was started with the ringing of the bell of the Basilica of Saint Francisco.
1825From that point of view, Bolivia was the last Spanish imperial territory in South America to gain its independence, in 1825.
1826It was proclaimed provisional capital of the newly independent Upper Peru (later, Bolivia) in July 1826.
1839On July 12, 1839, President José Miguel de Velasco proclaimed a law naming the city as the capital of Bolivia, and renaming it in honor of the revolutionary leader Antonio José de Sucre.
1898After the economic decline of Potosí and its silver industry, the Bolivian seat of government was moved from Sucre to La Paz in 1898.
