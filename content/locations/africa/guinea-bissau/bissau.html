City history

1680In 1680 Bissau even helped the Portuguese in a conflict with the Papels of Cacheu.
1687The city was founded in 1687 as a Portuguese trading post.
1696In response the Portuguese Conselho Ultramarino established the captaincy-general of Bissau, and by 1696 the town had a fort, a church, and a hospital.
1775The fort was rebuilt by the Grão Pará and Maranhão Company in 1775 to better project Portuguese power and store more slaves for shipment to Brazil.
1869In 1869, as part of an effort to more efficiently govern the territory, Bissau was raised to the status of commune.
20th centuryThe decades on either side of the turn of the 20th century saw fierce resistance on the part of the Papels to colonial 'pacification campaigns.' In 1915, after 30 years of war, the Portuguese.
1941In 1941 the capital was transferred from Bolama to Bissau.
1973After the declaration of independence by the anti-colonial guerrillas of PAIGC in 1973, the capital of the rebel territories was declared to be Madina do Boe, while Bissau remained the colonial capital.
16th centuryWhen the Portuguese began to trade there in the 16th century, the king of Bissau was among the most supportive monarchs of the region.
Civic roleAccording to oral tradition, the kingdom was founded by Mecau, the son of the king of Quinara (Guinala), who moved to the area with his pregnant sister, six wives, and subjects of his father's.
Regional roleAlthough the king of Bissau Bacompolco refused them permission to build a fort, he did grant them a trading factory, from which they shipped thousands of slaves, among other things.
Regional roleIt was the main emporium for trade on and south of the Geba river, and was rivaling if not eclipsing Cacheu in importance.
Regional roleWhen Captain-General Pinheiro tried to enforce Portugal's monopoly in defiance of the Papel policy of free trade, Incinhate surrounded the incomplete fort and threatened to massacre the inhabitants.
Regional roleUnable to enforce a trading monopoly or collect duties from foreign shipping, the Portuguese soon abandoned the fort.
