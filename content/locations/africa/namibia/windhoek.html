City history

500He built a stone church that held 500 people; it was also used as a school.
1840The first known mention of the name Windhoek was in a letter from Jonker Afrikaner to Joseph Tindall, dated 12 August 1844. Early settlement In 1840 Jonker Afrikaner established an Orlam settlement at Windhoek.
1842Two Rhenish missionaries, Carl Hugo Hahn and Franz Heinrich Kleinschmidt, started working there in late 1842.
1873After a long absence, Hahn visited Windhoek again in 1873 and was dismayed to see that nothing remained of the town's former prosperity.
1884A request by merchants from Lüderitzbucht resulted in the declaration in 1884 of a German protectorate over what was called German South West Africa (Deutsch-Südwestafrika), now Namibia.
1885In June 1885, a Swiss botanist found only jackals and starving guinea fowl amongst neglected fruit trees.
1890Colonial Windhoek was founded on 18 October 1890, when von François fixed the foundation stone of the fort, which is now known as the Alte Feste (Old Fortress).
1907After 1907, development accelerated as indigenous people migrated from the countryside to the growing town to seek work.
1920In 1920, after the Treaty of Versailles, the territory was placed under a League of Nations Class C mandate and again administered by South Africa.
1971In 1971, the Namibian general contract workers strike started from Windhoek with the goal of abolishing the contract labour system, opposing apartheid, and promoting Namibia's independence. Since Namibian independence Since independence in 1990, Windhoek.
