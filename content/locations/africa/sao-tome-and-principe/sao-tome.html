City history

1470The island was uninhabited before the arrival of the Portuguese sometime around 1470.
1493Álvaro Caminha founded the colony of São Tomé in 1493.
1497In 1497, 2,000 Jewish children, eight years old and under, were kidnapped from the Iberian peninsula, and forcefully converted to receive catholic education, following the national policy of conversion to Catholicism.
1566Another early building is Fort São Sebastião, built in 1566 and now the São Tomé National Museum.
1596On 9 July 1595, a slave revolt led by Rei Amador took control of the capital; they were subjugated in 1596.
1599In 1599, the Dutch took the city as well as the islands for two days; they re-occupied it in 1641 for a year.
1600The island of São Tomé was the main center of sugar production in the sixteenth century; it was overtaken by Brazil by 1600.
1975The city served as the capital of the Portuguese colony of São Tomé and Príncipe and, from São Tomé and Príncipe's independence in 1975, as capital of the sovereign nation.
19th centurySão Tomé is centred on a sixteenth-century cathedral, that was largely rebuilt in the 19th century.
GrowthThe Portuguese came to São Tomé in search of land to grow sugarcane.
Civic roleThe nearby African Kingdom of Kongo eventually became a source of slave labor as well.
