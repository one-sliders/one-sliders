City history

1497In 1497, Portuguese explorer Vasco da Gama recorded a sighting of the Cape of Good Hope.
1510In 1510, at the Battle of Salt River, the Portuguese admiral Francisco de Almeida and sixty-four of his men were killed and his party was defeated by the "Goringhaiqua" (in Dutch approximate spelling) using.
1870sIn 1859 the first railway line was built by the Cape Government Railways and a system of railways rapidly expanded in the 1870s.
1867The discovery of diamonds in Griqualand West in 1867, and the Witwatersrand Gold Rush in 1886, prompted a flood of immigration into South Africa.
1895In 1895 the city's first public power station, the Graaff Electric Lighting Works, was opened.
1899Conflicts between the Boer republics in the interior and the British colonial government resulted in the Second Boer War of 1899–1902.
1945In 1945 the expansion of the Cape Town foreshore was completed, adding an additional 194 ha (480 acres) to the Cape Town CBD, and by extension, the City Bowl region. Apartheid era Prior to.
1965After it was declared a whites-only area in 1965, all housing there was demolished and over 60,000 residents were forcibly removed.
2014In 2014 Cape Town was named World Design Capital of the Year.
2023Cape Town was voted the best tourist destination in Africa at the 2023 World Travel Awards in Dubai and continues to be the most important tourist destination in the country.
16th centuryIn the late 16th century French, Danish, Dutch, and English, but mainly Portuguese, ships regularly continued to stop over in Table Bay en route to the Indies.
1800sThe city became the capital of the newly formed Cape Colony, whose territory expanded very substantially through the 1800s, partially as a result of numerous wars with the amaXhosa on the colony's eastern frontier.
Regional roleIt was later renamed by John II of Portugal as "Cape of Good Hope" (Cabo da Boa Esperança) because of the great optimism engendered by the opening of a sea route to the Indian.
Regional roleCape Town became the legislative capital of the Union, and later of the Republic of South Africa.
