City history

150At first, all goods arriving by sea or being sent by sea had to be carried by porters between Léopoldville and Matadi, the port below the rapids and 150 km (93 mi) from the.
300The post flourished as the first navigable port on the Congo River above Livingstone Falls, a series of rapids over 300 kilometres (190 miles) below Leopoldville.
1881The city was established as a trading post by Henry Morton Stanley in 1881.
1890A major urban expansion was the construction of the Matadi–Léopoldville Railway, which was initiated in 1890 and completed in 1911.
1898The completion of the Matadi–Kinshasa portage railway, in 1898, provided an alternative route around the rapids and sparked the rapid development of Léopoldville.
1911In 1911, George Moulaert, an influential colonial administrator, drafted a comprehensive urban plan for Léopoldville and the greater Kinshasa area that would guide spatial development for decades, while World War I accelerated local economic.
1914In 1914, a pipeline was installed so that crude oil could be transported from Matadi to the upriver steamers in Leopoldville.
1923By 1923, the city was elevated to capital of the Belgian Congo, replacing the town of Boma in the Congo estuary, pursuant to the Royal Decree of 1 July 1923, countersigned by the Minister.
1929This transition, finalized in 1929, led to the development of a new administrative quartier located between Kinshasa, then emerging as a major commercial center, and Léopoldville-West, a preexisting settlement.
1954Lovanium University, the colony's inaugural university, was founded in 1954.
1966On 2 May 1966, the government announced that the nation's major cities would be restored to their pre-colonial names, effective on 30 June, the sixth anniversary of independence.
OriginsIn pre-colonial times, the area was inhabited by two trading centres, Ntamo and Ntsaasa, which were part of the Tio Kingdom.
Regional roleLéopoldville then became predominantly inhabited by the Bakongo ethnic group.
Regional roleThe park also contained vegetable gardens tended by unemployed volunteers, while immediately south of the neutral zone lay the indigenous area, which encompassed key facilities such as Ndolo Airport, Kinshasa Central Market, Kinshasa General.
