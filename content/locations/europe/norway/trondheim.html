City history

997The city was established in 997 by Olav Tryggvason and it was originally named Nidaros (Old Norse: Niðaróss).
1152From 1152, Trondheim was the seat of the Archbishop of Nidaros for Norway, which operated from the Archbishop's Palace.
1217It was also frequently used as the king's seat and was Norway's capital until 1217.
1651The 1651 fire destroyed 90% of all buildings within the city limits.
1658After the Treaty of Roskilde on 26 February 1658, Trondheim and the rest of Trøndelag became Swedish territory briefly.
1681After the "Horneman Fire" in 1681, there was an almost total reconstruction of the city, overseen by General Johan Caspar von Cicignon, originally from Luxembourg.
1838The city of Trondheim was established on 1 January 1838 (see formannskapsdistrikt law).
1924In 1924, the name of the Norwegian capital city was changed from Kristiania to Oslo, removing the name referencing a Danish King in favor of the very old name Oslo.
1929The Trondhjem Cathedral would be changed to Nidaros Cathedral effective on 1 July 1929 and the name of the city would change to Nidaros starting on 1 January 1930.
1940During the Second World War, Trondheim was occupied by Nazi Germany from 9 April 1940, the first day of the invasion of Norway, until the end of the war in Europe, 8 May 1945.
13th centuryThe scales symbolise justice and the motif is based on the political philosophy of the 13th century, where the balance of power between king and church was an important issue.
Regional roleDuring the late Middle Ages, the city name was commonly shortened to Þróndheimr, dropping the kaupanger part, and over time the name became Trondhjem, using the Dano-Norwegian spelling rather than the Old Norse spelling.
Regional roleSome scholars believe that the famous Lewis chessmen, 12th-century chess pieces carved from walrus ivory that were found in the Hebrides and are now at the British Museum, may have been made in Trondheim.
Regional roleBroad avenues, such as Munkegata, were created, without regard for private property rights, to limit the damage from future fires.
