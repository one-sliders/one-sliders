City history

1134It is unclear when Roskilde became a market town but it was certainly enjoying trading privileges under King Eric II who reigned from 1134 to 1137.
1135In 1135 the town was destroyed by the Pomeranian duke Ratibor I.
1150In 1150, Sweyn Grathe built a moat around the city.
1151In 1151/2, a religious confraternity was founded for the defence of the town against Wendish pirates.
1170Absalon, the Danish bishop, had a brick church built on the site of Harald's church in 1170.
1186The Roskilde bishops owned large areas of land in the region including, from 1186, Havn on the Øresund which later became Copenhagen.
1268These privileges were firmly established when the Roskilde City Council granted market town status to other towns on Zealand on 15 June 1268.
1275Today's cathedral was completed in 1275 after five of Absalon's successors had contributed to its construction.
11th centuryFrom the 11th century until 1443, it was the capital of Denmark.
1835Conditions improved in 1835 when the city became the Assembly of the Estates of the Realm (Rådgivende Stænderforsamling) and in 1847 with the railway connecting Copenhagen and Roskilde.
1970sIn the 1970s, the city benefited from the Holbæk Motorway which linked it to Copenhagen and the establishment of Roskilde University in 1972.
Modern cityBy the Middle Ages, with the support of kings and bishops, it had become one of the most important centres in Scandinavia.
Regional roleAs a result of Absalon's influence, many other churches were built in the vicinity, making Roskilde the most important town in Zealand.
Regional roleWith the development of the rail network, Roskilde became an important hub for traffic with Copenhagen.
