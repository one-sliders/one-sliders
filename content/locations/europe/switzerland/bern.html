City history

1191According to 14th-century historiography (Cronica de Berno, 1309), Bern was founded in 1191 by Berthold V, Duke of Zähringen.
1218In 1218, after Berthold died without an heir, Bern was made a free imperial city by the Goldene Handfeste of Holy Roman Emperor Frederick II. Old Swiss Confederacy In 1353, Bern joined the Swiss.
1405After a major blaze in 1405, the city's original wooden buildings were gradually replaced by half-timbered houses, and subsequently the sandstone buildings which came to be characteristic for the Old Town.
1415Bern invaded and conquered Aargau in 1415 and Vaud in 1536, as well as other smaller territories, thereby becoming the largest city-state north of the Alps.
1622It was succeeded by the Christoffelturm (formerly located closer to the site of the modern-day railway station) until 1622.
1848On 28 November 1848, during the revolutions that year, a majority of the new Swiss Federal Assembly deputies chose Bern as the Federal City (seat of the government) of the newly created Swiss federal.
1979At this time, it once again became the largest canton of the Confederacy as it stood during the Restoration and until the secession of the canton of Jura in 1979.
12th centuryNo archaeological evidence that indicates a settlement on the site of today's city centre prior to the 12th century has been found so far.
18th centuryBy the 18th century, it comprised most of what is today the canton of Bern and the canton of Vaud.
19th centuryThe city was originally built on a hilly peninsula surrounded by the river Aare, but outgrew natural boundaries by the 19th century.
Regional roleIn antiquity, a Celtic oppidum stood on the Engehalbinsel (peninsula) north of Bern, fortified since the second century BC (late La Tène period), thought to be one of the 12 oppida of the Helvetii.
Regional roleDuring the Thirty Years' War, two new fortifications – the so-called big and small Schanze (entrenchments) – were built to protect the whole area of the peninsula.
Regional roleIn addition, Bern had a more central location and was supported by the French-speaking cantons due to proximity to them.
Regional roleA number of congresses of the socialist First and Second Internationals were held in Bern, particularly during World War I when Switzerland was neutral; see Bern International.
