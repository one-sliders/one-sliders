1320sIn the 1320s, the Mongols retreated from Georgia, and Tbilisi became the capital of an independent Georgian state, once again.
Regional roleArabic dirhams were brought to Georgia following the Arab conquest in the seventh century, and a mint was founded in Tbilisi that produced coins with inscriptions in both Arabic and Georgian.
Regional roleDavid moved his residence from Kutaisi to Tbilisi, making it the capital of a unified Georgian State, thus inaugurating the Georgian Golden Age.
Regional roleThe city also became an important literary and cultural center, not only for Georgia, but also for the Eastern Orthodox world of the time.
Regional roleThe nation itself maintained a form of "semi-independence" and did not lose its statehood, but Tbilisi would be strongly influenced by the Mongols for the next century, both politically and culturally.
Regional roleDuring this period, many parts of Tbilisi were reconstructed and rebuilt.
Regional roleRussian Imperial administrators implemented a new Western-style city plan and commissioned new buildings and infrastructure, including roads and railroads connecting Tbilisi to other important cities in the Russian Empire, such as Batumi and Poti.
Regional roleThe main new artery built under Russian administration was Golovin Avenue (present-day Rustaveli Avenue), on which the Viceroys of the Caucasus established their residence.
Regional roleIt was in the former Viceroy of the Caucasus's palace where the independence of three Transcaucasus nations – Georgia, Armenia and Azerbaijan – was declared by their respective national councils on 26 to 28.
