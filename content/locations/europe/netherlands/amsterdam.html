City history

100More than 100,000 Dutch Jews were deported and murdered in Nazi concentration camps, including 56,521 victims in Auschwitz and a further number of 34,082 accounted for in Sobibor.
1170A settlement was built here immediately after the landscape change of 1170.
1275The settlement first appeared in a document from 1275, concerning a road toll granted by the count of Holland Floris V to the residents apud Amestelledamme 'at the dam in the Amstel' or 'at.
1345The Mirakel van Amsterdam in 1345 rendered the city an important place of pilgrimage.
1602In 1602, the Amsterdam office of the Dutch East India Company became the world's first stock exchange by trading in its own shares.
1814The city was a major destination port for Dutch slave ships participating in the triangular trade, which lasted until the United Netherlands abolished the Netherlands' involvement in the trade in 1814 at the request.
20th centuryIn 1906, Joseph Conrad gave a brief description of Amsterdam as seen from the seaside, in The Mirror of the Sea. 20th century – present Shortly before the First World War, the city started.
1916On 1 January 1921, after a flood in 1916, the depleted municipalities of Durgerdam, Holysloot, Zunderdorp and Schellingwoude (all lying north of Amsterdam) were, at their own request, annexed to the city.
1943The two main waves of arrest, culminating in deportation, occurred on 26 May 1943 and on 20 June 1943.
15th centuryFrom the 15th century on the city established an independent trade route with the Baltic Sea in grain and timber, cutting out the Hanseatic League as middlemen.
17th centuryFrom the 17th century onwards, Amsterdam also became involved in the Atlantic slave trade.
