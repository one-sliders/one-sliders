City history

980By 980, the area was incorporated into the early medieval Principality of Polotsk, one of the earliest East Slavic principalities of Kievan Rus'.
1067City authorities consider the date of 3 March 1067 to be the exact founding date of the city, though the town (by then fortified by wooden walls) had certainly existed for some time.
1129In 1129, the Principality of Minsk was annexed by Kiev, the dominant principality of Kievan Rus'; however in 1146 the Polotsk dynasty regained control of the principality.
1242In 1242, Minsk became a part of the expanding Grand Duchy of Lithuania.
1796In 1796, it became the centre of the Minsk Governorate.
1836A first public library was opened in 1836, and a fire brigade was put into operation in 1837.
1871In 1871, a railway link between Moscow and Warsaw ran via Minsk, and in 1873, a new railway from Romny in Ukraine to the Baltic Sea port of Libava (Liepāja) was also constructed.
1917A Workers' Soviet was established in Minsk in October 1917, drawing much of its support from disaffected soldiers and workers.
1918On 25 March 1918, Minsk was proclaimed the capital of the Belarusian People's Republic.
1924By 1924, there were 29 factories in operation; schools, museums, theatres and libraries were also established.
1942Some anti-Soviet residents of Minsk, who hoped that Belarus could regain independence, did support the Germans, especially at the beginning of the occupation, but by 1942, Minsk had become a major centre of the.
16th centuryBy the middle of the 16th century, Minsk was an important economic and cultural centre in the Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth.
1990sIts surrounding villages were absorbed and rebuilt as mikroraions, districts of high-density apartment housing. Recent developments Throughout the 1990s, after the fall of communism, the city continued to change.
Regional roleThus Minsk became an important rail junction and a manufacturing hub.
