City history

395In 395, following the division of the Roman Empire into two, Scupi became part of the Eastern Roman Empire.
518In 518, Scupi was destroyed by a violent earthquake, possibly the most devastating the town had ever experienced.
972It served as the Bulgarian capital from 972 to 992, and Tsar Samuel ruled it from 976 until 1004, when its governor surrendered it to Byzantine Emperor Basil the Bulgar Slayer in 1004 in.
1203Kaloyan brought Skopje back into the re-established Second Bulgarian Empire in 1203 until his nephew Strez declared autonomy along the Upper Vardar with Serbian aid only five years later.
1451The Stone Bridge, "one of the most imposing stone bridges to be found in Yugoslavia", was built under the patronage of Sultan Mehmed II the Conqueror between 1451 and 1469.
1492Mustafa Pasha Mosque, built in 1492, is reputed to be "one of the most resplendent sacral Islamic buildings in the Balkans." In 1535 all churches were demolished by decree of the Ottoman governor.
1870During the Tanzimat reforms, nationalism arose in the Ottoman Empire and in 1870 a new Bulgarian Exarchate was established and its separate diocese was created, based on ethnic identity, rather than religious principles.
1929After the end of the World War, Vardar Macedonia became part of the new Kingdom of Serbs, Croats, and Slovenes, which became "Kingdom of Yugoslavia" in 1929.
1944Skopje became the capital city of the newly proclaimed Democratic Federal Macedonia as set up by the ASNOM on 2 August 1944 in the Bulgarian occupation zone in Yugoslavia.
14th centuryIn the 14th century, Skopje became such an important city that King Stefan Dušan made it the capital of the Serbian Empire.
Regional roleThe first church was founded under the reign of Emperor Constantine the Great and Scupi became the seat of a diocese.
Regional roleUnder the political stability of the Nemanjić rule, the settlement spread outside the walls of the fortress, towards Gazi Baba hill.
Regional roleChurches, monasteries and markets were built and tradesmen from the Republic of Venice and Dubrovnik opened shops.
Regional roleThe railway station was built south of the Vardar and this contributed to the relocation of economic activities on that side of the river, which had never been urbanised before.
