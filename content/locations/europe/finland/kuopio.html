City history

1653Governor Per Brahe the Younger founded the settlement of Kuopio in 1653.
1809Following the conclusion of the Finnish War in 1809, which occurred in the context of the broader Napoleonic Wars, Finland would be annexed by the Russian Empire as an autonomous Grand Duchy.
1856Major public works projects built during this time include the Saimaa Canal in 1856, which opened up a summer route between Lake Saimaa and the Baltic Sea, and the Savo railway in 1889, which.
1940sAt the end of the 1940s, support was also provided for school transports and the provision of free school meals, expanding a welfare program that had been active since 1902.
1934Kuopio's original train station was built the same year and would serve as the city's main rail terminus until 1934, when it was demolished replaced by the current Kuopio railway station.
1957The current design was approved by the Kuopio City Council at a meeting on November 25, 1957 and was confirmed by the Ministry of the Interior on June 12, 1958.
2015The municipality of Maaninka joined the city of Kuopio in 2015, the town of Nilsiä in 2013, and Karttula in 2011, Vehmersalmi in 2005, Riistavesi in 1973, and Kuopion maalaiskunta in 1969.
16th centuryOne theory proposes that in the 16th century, a local notable named Kauhanen in Tavinsalmi, a village approximately 32 km to the northwest of the city, changed his name to Skopa.
1550sIn the 1550s, the Finnish Church, under the influence of Protestant reformer Mikael Agricola, established parish church in Kuopionniemi.
19th centuryIn the 19th century, Eastern Finland would see considerable infrastructural investment and modernization, particularly in the realm of transportation.
1960sIn the 1960s, the construction of suburbs began with the development of Puijonlaakso.
Regional roleThe development of infrastructure would lead to the further economic integration of previously isolated, inland areas, with economic and commercial hubs on the Baltic Coast.
Regional roleFollowing World War II, Kuopio's population would grow significantly due in part to the settlement of Karelian evacuees and a high birth rate.
