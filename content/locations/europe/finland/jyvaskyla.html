City history

21st centuryDuring the 21st century Jyväskylä has grown fast – by over 1,000 inhabitants every year.
1506However, the name Jyväsjoki was registered in 1506 for the region's first known resident, Heikki Ihanninpoika Jyväsjoki.
1539According to the oldest available taxation documents (maakirja), there were seven estates on the Jyväskylä region in 1539.
1565A shorter form of the name, Jyväs (written Jyuexe) was mentioned slightly earlier in 1565 and again (this time written Jyues) in 1570.
1600The oldest estate in Jyväskylä continuously held by the same family is the estate of Lahti, which emerged when the estate of Mattila was split between two brothers in 1600.
1837The City of Jyväskylä was founded on 22 March 1837, when Emperor of Russia and Grand Duke of Finland, Nicholas I of Russia, signed the charter of the city and the infrastructure was essentially.
1858The first three Finnish-speaking schools in the world were founded in Jyväskylä, the lycée in 1858, the teachers’ college in 1863, and the girls’ school in 1864.
1993Säynätsalo was consolidated with Jyväskylä in 1993, and Jyväskylän maalaiskunta and Korpilahti, for their part, on January 1, 2009.
20th centuryIn the early 20th century, the town expanded several times.
Regional roleHis house was located at the mouth of the Äijälänjoki River, which in Fredrikson's opinion also supports the Syväsjoki theory.
Regional roleIn the Jyväskylä region, there are archeological findings from the Stone Age.
Regional roleThe history of the Lahti family and estate have had a significant impact on the development of Jyväskylä region.
Regional roleThe original town was built between Lake Jyväsjärvi (which is connected to Lake Päijänne) and the Jyväskylä ridge (Harju), and consisted of most of the current grid-style city centre.
Regional roleMost of today's Jyväskylä was built after the Continuation War, when evacuees from ceded territories were settled in the city, and housing was badly needed.
