City history

1154Early literary sources such as Al-Idrisi's world map from 1154 mention Turku. Swedish era According to the permission granted by Pope Gregory IX on 23 January 1229, the episcopal seat was moved from Nousiainen.
16th centuryAt the beginning of the 16th century, in connection with the disputes of the Kalmar Union, the Danes destroyed the city twice, in 1509 under the leadership of Admiral Otte Rud and in 1522.
1640In 1640, the first university in Finland, the Royal Academy of Turku, was founded in Turku by order of Queen Christina.
1827The government offices that remained in Turku were finally moved to the new capital after the Great Fire of Turku, which destroyed a large portion of the city in 1827.
19th centuryIn the middle of the 19th century, Turku was, after Helsinki, the most important craft city in Finland, but the Industrial Revolution with steam and electric machines was experienced in Turku only around 1900.
11th centuryIn the 11th century, the Turku region began to develop as a port.
12th centurySome cemeteries were utilized during the initial migratory phase, while some were solely utilized during the Viking Age, and others were established in the 12th century.
14th centuryIn the 14th century, two-thirds of the city's burghers were German, but gradually the proportion of domestic burghers increased.
Regional roleThere is nothing to suggest that the actual city of Turku still existed at this point; however, the city was not founded on uninhabited land, but there were fields and probably also a peasant.
Regional roleEven if Turku had no official capital status, it was for a long time the most important city in Finland as part of the trade and shipping of the Hanseatic League.
Regional roleTurku became briefly the official capital, but soon lost the status to Helsinki, as Emperor Alexander I felt that Turku was too far from Russia and too aligned with Sweden to serve as the.
Regional roleAfter the fire, a new and safer city plan was drawn up by German architect Carl Ludvig Engel, who had also designed the new capital, Helsinki.
Regional roleThe First World War provided a boost to the city's industry, as the export difficulties affected the wood industry, which Turku didn't have much of, and it was easy to get much-needed raw materials.
Regional roleTwo years later, the Finnish-language University of Turku was founded alongside it.
