City history

Regional role1,800 Gotlanders were killed in battle in front of the city.
13th centuryIt was reshaped in the 13th century to its current appearance, and was officially opened in 1225, by the bishop of the Swedish city of Linköping (in regional map).
15th centuryAround 1300, it was rebuilt to reach its current height, acquiring the characteristic towers, although some towers were not constructed until the 15th century.
1361In 1361, Gotland was conquered by Valdemar IV of Denmark.
1391In 1391, 1394 and 1398, it was taken and plundered by the Victual Brothers, pirates who sailed the Baltic Sea.
1398An invading army of Teutonic Knights conquered Gotland in 1398, destroyed Visby and expelled the Victual Brothers.
1409In 1409, Grand Master Ulrich von Jungingen of the Teutonic Knights guaranteed peace with the Kalmar Union of Scandinavia by selling the island of Gotland to Queen Margaret of Denmark, Norway and Sweden.
1411In 1411, the Norwegian, Danish and Swedish King Eric of Pomerania had the castle of Visborg constructed, and settled himself there for 12 years, during which the city virtually became a pirates' nest, and.
12th centuryIn the 12th century, Visby Cathedral, dedicated to Saint Mary, was constructed.
14th centuryIn the Gutasagan (mid 14th century) the place is referred to as just Wi meaning "holy place, place of worship".
Civic roleIt was inhabited as early as the Stone Age, probably because of the access to fresh water and a natural harbour.
Regional roleIn the Danish throne quarrel, Lübeck, a Free City of the Holy Roman Empire and a leading member of the Hanseatic League, supported Frederick I, while Søren Norby the Danish governor of Gotland fought.
Regional roleWhile Norby fought a military action in Sweden, the Lübeckers successfully attacked Visby and set the city on fire from four sites.
Regional roleMary's Cathedral remained the last functional church and became the new city parish.
