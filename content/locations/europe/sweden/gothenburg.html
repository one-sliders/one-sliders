City history

1603The church was built in 1603 and destroyed in 1611.
1621After several failed attempts, Gothenburg was successfully founded in 1621 by King Gustavus Adolphus (Gustaf II Adolf).
1652The Dutchmen initially won political power, and it was not until 1652, when the last Dutch politician in the city's council died, that Swedes acquired political power over Gothenburg.
1731However, in 1731, the Swedish East India Company was founded, and the city flourished due to its foreign trade with highly profitable commercial expeditions to China.
1807In 1807, a decision was made to tear down most of the city's wall.
1810The work started in 1810 and was carried out by 150 soldiers from the Bohus regiment.
1841In 1841, the Scotsman Alexander Keiller founded the Götaverken shipbuilding company that was in business until 1989.
1906His son James Keiller donated Keiller Park to the city in 1906.
18th centuryIn the 18th century, fishing was the most important industry.
Modern cityThe site of the first church built in Gothenburg, subsequently destroyed by Danish invaders, is marked by a stone near the north end of the Älvsborg Bridge in the Färjenäs Park.
Regional roleThe planning of the streets and canals of Gothenburg closely resembled that of Jakarta, which was built by the Dutch around the same time.
Regional roleWilliam Chalmers, the son of a Scottish immigrant, donated his fortunes to set up what later became the Chalmers University of Technology.
Regional roleGothenburg grew into a significant port and trade centre on the west coast, because it was the only city on the west coast that, along with Marstrand, was granted the rights to trade with.
Regional roleThe impact of Gothenburg as a main port of embarkation for Swedish emigrants is reflected by Gothenburg, Nebraska, a small Swedish settlement in the United States.
