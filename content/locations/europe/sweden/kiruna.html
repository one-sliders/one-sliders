City history

1736The ore became better known after it was reported by Mangi, a Sámi man, in 1736 to Swedish authorities that had gathered in Jukkasjärvi Church.
1884In 1884, a concession for a railway from Luleå to Narvik was granted to The Northern Europe Railway Company.
1888The provisional railway between Luleå and Malmberget was finished in 1888 and the first train left Malmberget in March.
1890At the initiative of Robert Schoug, the Luossavaara-Kiirunavaara Aktiebolag (LKAB) was founded in 1890.
1893In 1893, Gustaf Broms became CEO of both LKAB and AGM.
1903However, official residences were built at a high pace, and when the king opened the railway in 1903, all illegal residences and most other provisional buildings had been demolished and replaced.
1908Kiruna became a municipalsamhälle (a community within a municipality) in 1908.
1912In return, the mining company LKAB paid for a hospital, fire station, sewerage, roads, a church (opened 1912) and the priest's home.
1920During World War I, iron ore production dropped to the lowest level in LKAB's history, and when exports increased again, a successful three-month strike in 1920 led to a 20% increase in salaries for.
1957In 1957, the Kiruna Geophysical Observatory (KGO) (now the Swedish Institute of Space Physics) was founded and established by the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences.
1960On 10 November 1960, Kiruna Airport opened to separate civilian air traffic from the military airplanes that had landed at Kalixfors airport and at Luossajärvi since World War I.
1990In 1990, the first Icehotel, rebuilt each year from blocks of ice, was built in Jukkasjärvi and advertised as the world's largest igloo.
1993In 1993, The Umeå space engineers moved to the same building housing IRF and a year later another Luleå University programme, civil engineering with specialisation in space technology, started at the same location.
Regional roleSwedish Railways had already run special trains before World War II, but started a special Dollar train in the summer months between Gothenburg and Kiruna, connecting to cruise ships from the United States.
