City history

1670The town that would be called Nassau was founded in 1670 by British noblemen who brought British settlers with them to New Providence.
1684In 1684 the town was burned to the ground during the Raid on Charles Town.
1689William was the Dutch Stadtholder (stadhouder in Dutch), and, from 1689, the King of England, Scotland and Ireland.
1695It was rebuilt in 1695 under Governor Nicholas Trott and renamed Nassau in honour of King William III, who belonged to a branch of the House of Nassau.
1703In 1703, Spanish and French allied forces briefly occupied Nassau.
1713Thomas Walker was the island's last remaining appointed official and although evidence is scarce, it appears that he was acting in the role of deputy governor upon Benjamin Hornigold's arrival in 1713.
1776In 1776, the Battle of Nassau resulted in a brief occupation by American Continental Marines during the American War of Independence, where the marines staged their first amphibious raid on Fort Montague after attempting.
1783In April 1783, Andrew Deveaux, an American Loyalist who resettled on the island, set forth and recaptured the island for the British Crown.
2300With funds from privateering, a new fort, street lights and over 2300 sumptuous houses were built and Nassau was extended.
GrowthThey built a fort, and named it Charles Town in honour of England's King Charles II.
Regional roleBenjamin Hornigold, along with his great rival Henry Jennings, became the unofficial overlord of a veritable pirate republic which played host to the self-styled Flying Gang.
Regional roleRogers cleaned up Nassau and rebuilt the fort, using his own wealth to try to overcome problems.
Regional roleHe oversaw the construction of Fort Charlotte and Fort Fincastle in Nassau.
Regional roleDuring the American Civil War, Nassau served as a port for blockade runners making their way to and from ports along the southern Atlantic Coast for continued trade with the Confederacy.
