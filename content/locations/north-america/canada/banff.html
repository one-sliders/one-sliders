City history

Early settlementThe site of Banff has been continually inhabited by First Nations for at least 10,000 years.
1883In 1883, three Canadian Pacific Railway workers stumbled upon a series of natural hot springs on the side of Sulphur Mountain.
1885In 1885, Canada established a federal reserve of 26 km2 (10 sq mi) around the Cave and Basin hot springs and began promoting the area as an international resort and spa as a way.
1887In 1887, the reserve area was increased to 673 km2 (260 sq mi) and named "Rocky Mountain Park".
1915An internment camp was set up at Banff and Castle Mountain in Dominion Park from July 1915 to July 1917, mostly imprisoning Ukrainian immigrants.
1976In 1976, the International Astronomical Union's Working Group for Planetary System Nomenclature (IAU/WGPSN) adopted the name Banff for a crater on Mars, after the town in Alberta.
1985In 1985, the United Nations declared Banff National Park, as one of the Canadian Rocky Mountain Parks, a UNESCO world heritage site.
1990It was administered by the Government of Canada's national parks system until 1990 when the Town of Banff became the only incorporated municipality within a Canadian national park.
1991In 1991, Banff hosted the 1991 Winter Deaflympics, the first and only year that the Deaflympics was hosted in Canada.
19th centuryServices, such as St George-in-the-Pines church, were constructed through the late 19th century.
1880sThe town of Banff was first established in the 1880s after the transcontinental railway was built through the Bow Valley.
GrowthThe town's location was the site of an Interior Salishan settlement, which was both a wintering village and a trading post.
Regional roleThe Banff townsite was developed near the railway station as a service centre for tourists visiting the park.
Regional roleHe published the Crag and Canyon newspaper, built the King Edward Hotel and the Lux Theatre, and founded the Sign of the Goat Curio Shop, which led to the development of the Luxton Museum.
