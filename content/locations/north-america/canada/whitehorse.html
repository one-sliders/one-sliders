City history

1883The surrounding area had seasonal fish camps and Frederick Schwatka, in 1883, observed the presence of a portage trail used to bypass Miles Canyon.
1896The discovery of gold in the Klondike in August 1896, by Skookum Jim, Tagish Charlie, and George Washington Carmack, set off a major change in the historical patterns of the region.
1897Early prospectors used the Chilkoot Pass, but by July 1897, crowds of neophyte prospectors had arrived via steamship and were camping at "White Horse".
1898The White Pass and Yukon Route narrow-gauge railway linking Skagway to Whitehorse had begun construction in May 1898.
1899By May 1899, construction had arrived at the south end of Bennett Lake.
1900It was only in June–July 1900 that construction finished the difficult Bennett Lake section itself, completing the entire route.
1901By 1901, the Whitehorse Star was already reporting on daily freight volumes.
1905On May 23, 1905, a small fire in the barber shop of the Windsor Hotel got out of control when the fire engine ran out of water, spreading throughout the city and causing $300,000.
1920In 1920, the first planes landed in Whitehorse and the first air mail was sent in November 1927.
1942Until 1942, rail, river, and air were the only way to get to Whitehorse, but in 1942 the US military decided an interior road would be safer to transfer troops and provisions between Alaska.
1950In 1950, the city was incorporated and by 1951 the population had doubled from its 1941 numbers.
1953On April 1, 1953, the city was designated the capital of the Yukon Territory when the seat was moved from Dawson City after the construction of the Klondike Highway.
Regional roleThe White Horse Restaurant and Inn was among the buildings destroyed, after its co-founder Frederick Trump had sold his shares and left the city.
Regional roleThe Canadian portion of the highway was only returned to Canadian sovereignty after the war.
