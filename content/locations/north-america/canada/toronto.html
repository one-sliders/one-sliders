City history

450Toronto Hydro estimated 450,000 people were without power after the storm and Toronto Pearson International Airport reported 126 millimetres (5.0 in) of rain had fallen over five hours, more than during Hurricane Hazel.
1750French traders founded Fort Rouillé in 1750 (the current Exhibition grounds were later developed there), but abandoned it in 1759 during the Seven Years' War.
1854Long-distance railway lines were constructed, including a route completed in 1854 linking Toronto with the Upper Great Lakes.
1859A distillery built by Gooderham and Worts from 1859 to 1861 became the country's largest whisky factory.
1865After this date, Quebec was designated as the capital until 1865 (two years before Canadian Confederation).
1867Toronto became the capital of the province of Ontario after its official creation in 1867.
1876Long before the Royal Military College of Canada was established in 1876, supporters of the concept proposed military colleges in Canada.
1904In that year, a half-century after the Great Fire of 1904, disaster struck the city again when Hurricane Hazel brought intense winds and flash flooding.
1921The public transit system passed into public ownership in 1921 as the Toronto Transportation Commission, later renamed the Toronto Transit Commission.
Regional roleAmerican soldiers destroyed much of the garrison and set fire to the parliament buildings during their five-day occupation.
Regional roleThe advent of the railway dramatically increased the numbers of immigrants arriving, commerce and industry, as had the Lake Ontario steamers and schooners entering port before.
Regional roleAs new migrants began to prosper, they moved to better housing in other areas, in what is now understood to be succession waves of settlement.
Regional roleIn the decades after World War II, refugees from war-torn Europe and Chinese job-seekers arrived, as well as construction labourers, particularly from Italy and Portugal.
