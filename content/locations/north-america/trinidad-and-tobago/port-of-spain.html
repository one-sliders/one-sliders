City history

1960sPort of Spain continued to grow in size and importance during the 19th and early 20th centuries, peaking in size in the 1960s at about 100,000 people.
1680The French naval commander Comte d'Estrées visited in 1680, and reported that there was no Port of Spain.
1690But in 1690, Spanish governor Don Sebastien de Roteta reported in writing to the King of Spain.
1757By 1757, the old capital, San José de Oruña (modern Saint Joseph), about 11 kilometres (7 mi) inland, had fallen into disrepair, and Governor Don Pedro de la Moneda transferred his seat to Port.
1787Ann's River, prone to flooding, was impeding the expansion of the town, Chacón had its course diverted in 1787 so that it ran to the east of the city, along the foot of the.
1803In 1803, Port of Spain began growing southwards, with the reclamation of the foreshore mudflats, using fill from the Laventille Hills.
1840sFurther major reclamation efforts took place in the 1840s, the 1870s, and in 1906.
1935In 1935, the Deep Water Harbour Scheme dredged the offshore area along Port of Spain's western neighbourhoods.
1958From 1958 to 1962, Port of Spain was the temporary capital of the West Indies Federation, though there were plans to build a new federal capital at Chaguaramas.
1990In July 1990, an extremist group held the prime minister and members of parliament hostage for 5 days while rioting and looting shook Port of Spain.
2005In 2005, there was a series of bombings in Port of Spain that caused injuries to bystanders.
OriginsPort of Spain was founded by the Spanish as Puerto España.
GrowthThe first settlement was near the site of the Amerindian fishing village of Cumucurapo ("place of the silk cotton trees"), located in the area today known as Mucurapo, west of the city centre.
Civic roleThe part of today's downtown Port of Spain closest to the sea was once an area of tidal mudflats covered by mangroves.
