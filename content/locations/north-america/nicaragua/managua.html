City history

Civic roleManagua (and the entire area of Nicaragua) was inhabited by Paleo-Indians as far back as 12,000 BC.
300More than 300,000 Nicaraguans returned from abroad bringing their expertise and needed capital.
1819Founded as a pre-Columbian fishing town, the city was incorporated in 1819 and given the name Leal Villa de Santiago de Managua.
1824Efforts to make Managua the capital of Nicaragua began in 1824, after the Central American nations formally attained their independence from Spain.
1852Hence, Managua was officially selected as the nation's capital in 1852.
1876The city was hampered by major floods in 1876 and 1885.
20th centuryToday's references differentiate the pre-1970s Managua by labeling it as La Antigua Ciudad, which in English translates to "The Ancient City". 20th century A disastrous earthquake in 1931 destroyed much of the city, killing.
1936A large fire in 1936 also destroyed much of the city.
1972Managua's progress came to a sudden halt after it suffered a second major earthquake on December 23, 1972, which destroyed 90% of the city's downtown and killed more than 19,120 people.
1940sThe city's development caught the attention of Irving Fields and Albert Gamse, who composed a musical piece about the city that became popular in the 1940s through the performances of Freddy Martin, Guy Lombardo.
21st centuryBusinesses mushroomed, new housing projects and schools were constructed, the airport was expanded and modernized, streets were widened, older malls were repaired and new ones were built, and buildings were cleaned up. 21st century.
Regional roleEscaping the city center, earthquake victims found refuge in the outskirts of the city.
Regional roleIn addition, corruption within the Somoza regime which allocated part of the relief funds hindered the reconstruction of the city's center which remains somewhat isolated from the rest of the capital.
Regional roleNew governmental buildings, galleries, museums, apartment buildings, squares, promenades, monuments, boat tours on Lake Managua, restaurants, nighttime entertainment, and broad avenues have resurrected part of downtown Managua's former vitality.
