City history

1730In 1730, three Catholic missions from East Texas were combined and reestablished as one mission on the south side of the Colorado River, in what is now Zilker Park, in Austin.
1839In 1839, the Texas Congress formed a commission to seek a site for the new capital of the Republic of Texas to replace Houston.
1871The opening of the Houston and Texas Central Railway (H&TC) in 1871 turned Austin into the major trading center for the region, with the ability to transport both cotton and cattle.
1940In 1940, the destroyed granite dam on the Colorado River was finally replaced by a hollow concrete dam that formed Lake McDonald (now called Lake Austin) and which has withstood all floods since.
19th centuryThe city grew throughout the 19th century and became a center for government and education with the construction of the Texas State Capitol and the University of Texas at Austin.
1870sHowever, as other new railroads were built through the region in the 1870s, Austin began to lose its primacy in trade to the surrounding communities.
20th centuryFollowing the mid-20th century, Austin became established as one of Texas' major metropolitan centers.
Civic roleSpanish colonists traveled through the area, though few permanent settlements were created for some time.
Regional roleSpanish forts were established in what are now Bastrop and San Marcos.
Regional roleFollowing Mexico's independence, new settlements were established in Central Texas.
Regional roleBlack communities such as Wheatville, Pleasant Hill, and Clarksville were established, with Clarksville being the oldest surviving freedomtown ‒ the original post-Civil War settlements founded by former African-American slaves ‒ west of the Mississippi.
Regional roleThe same year, Tillotson Collegiate and Normal Institute (now part of Huston–Tillotson University) opened its doors.
Regional roleIn addition, the state legislature established the Lower Colorado River Authority (LCRA) that, along with the city of Austin, created the system of dams along the Colorado River to form the Highland Lakes.
Regional roleIn addition, the much larger Mansfield Dam was built by the LCRA upstream of Austin to form Lake Travis, a flood-control reservoir.
