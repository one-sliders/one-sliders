City history

168918th and 19th centuries In 1689, French-Canadian trader Martin Chartier established a trading post on the Cumberland River, near the present-day site of the city.
1714In 1714, a group of French traders under the command of Charles Charleville established a settlement and trading post at the present location of downtown Nashville, which became known as French Lick.
1779In 1779, a settling party organized by Judge Richard Henderson, led by explorers James Robertson and John Donelson of Overmountain Men, traveled to the site of French Lick, and constructed Fort Nashborough.
1806In 1806, Nashville was incorporated as a city and became the county seat of Davidson County, Tennessee.
1843In 1843, the city was named as the permanent capital of the state of Tennessee.
1862In February 1862, Nashville became the first Confederate state capital to fall to U.S. troops, and the state was occupied by the U.S.
1868In 1868, three years after the end of the Civil War, the Nashville chapter of the Ku Klux Klan was founded by Confederate veteran John W.
1873In 1873, Nashville suffered another cholera epidemic, along with towns throughout Sumner County along railroad routes and the Cumberland River.
1894In 1894, the first chapter of United Daughters of the Confederacy was founded in the city, and it hosted the first two conventions of the organization.

Regional roleNashville quickly grew because of its strategic location as a port on the Cumberland River, a tributary of the Ohio River; and its later status as a major railroad center.
Regional roleFor years, Nashville was considered one of the wealthiest southern capitals and a large portion of its prominence was from the iron business.
