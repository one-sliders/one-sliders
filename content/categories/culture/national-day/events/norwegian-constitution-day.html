Why Norway celebrates 17 May

On 17 May 1814, Norway's founding fathers gathered at Eidsvoll and signed the constitution — one of the most liberal in the world at the time. The date marks the birth of Norway as a modern independent state, even though full independence from Sweden did not come until 1905.

The day quickly became the most important national celebration in Norway. Children's parades — Barnetoget — were established in the 1800s and are now the defining feature of the day. Thousands of school children march through every Norwegian town and city, led by school bands, waving flags and wearing their best clothes.

In Oslo, the parade passes the Royal Palace, where the royal family appears on the balcony to greet the crowds for hours. It is a rare occasion where the celebration belongs entirely to children and ordinary citizens rather than military displays.

Across Norway, the day follows a shared rhythm: bunad (national costume), flags, school breakfasts, parades, ice cream, hot dogs and family gatherings. The celebration is local and personal — every town does it slightly differently, but the spirit is the same everywhere.

The Norwegian flag

The Norwegian flag has a red field with a blue Nordic cross outlined in white. The design has been in use since 1821 and reflects Norway's ties with the other Nordic countries. On 17 May the flag is carried by almost every person in the parade and flies from homes, shops and public buildings across the country.

17 May falls in late spring, and the food reflects a day spent mostly outside. Meals are quick, portable and easy to share between parade stops.

Norwegian Constitution Day traditions

17 May is Norway's most visible public celebration. The streets fill with flags, school bands and people in national costume — it is one of the few days where the whole country participates at once.
